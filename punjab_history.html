History of Punjab

A Journey Through Time (1469 – Present)

1469 – 1708

The Sikh Gurus Period

This foundational era began with the birth of Guru Nanak Dev ji (1469). Key events include the compilation of the Adi Granth (1604) by Guru Arjan Dev ji and the founding of the Khalsa (1699) by Guru Gobind Singh ji, which shaped Sikh identity and principles.

1710 – 1799

Rise of Sikh Power & Misls

Following the Gurus, Banda Singh Bahadur led significant military campaigns (1710-1715). The period was marked by struggles against Mughals and Afghans, including the devastating Ghalugharas (massacres). The Sikhs organized into Misls (confederacies), which controlled Punjab by 1767.

1799 – 1839

The Sikh Empire

Maharaja Ranjit Singh, born in 1780, unified the Sikh Misls and established a vast, secular empire. He captured Lahore in 1799 and his rule is considered a golden age of stability, military strength, and prosperity in Punjab. He signed the Treaty of Amritsar with the British in 1809.

1839 – 1849

Anglo-Sikh Wars & British Annexation

After Ranjit Singh's death in 1839, political instability led to two major conflicts with the British. The First (1845-46) and Second (1848-49) Anglo-Sikh Wars resulted in the defeat of the Sikh army and the British annexation of Punjab on March 29, 1849.

1947

Partition of British India

With the end of British rule, the Punjab province was brutally dismembered. The division created West Punjab (in Pakistan) and East Punjab (in India), leading to one of the largest and most violent mass migrations in human history.

1966

Formation of Modern Punjab

On November 1, based on the Punjabi Suba movement, the Indian state of Punjab was reorganized on linguistic lines. This created the new states of Haryana and Himachal Pradesh, with Chandigarh becoming a Union Territory and the shared capital of Punjab and Haryana.
