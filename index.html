In-Depth Principles

1. Bernoulli’s Principle

Along a streamline in steady, incompressible, non‑viscous flow, the sum of static pressure, dynamic pressure (½ρv²) and hydrostatic head (ρgh) is constant.

Intuition: Fast‑moving fluid “spends” pressure energy on kinetic energy; slow fluid “gains” pressure.

Representative Applications:

  • Aircraft Wings: Faster air over the top surface lowers pressure, generating lift.
  • Venturi Meters: Calculate flow rate from pressure drop in a constricted pipe.
  • Atomizers & Sprayers: Low pressure entrains and atomizes liquid.
  • Pitot Tubes: Convert velocity into a measurable pressure difference.

2. Archimedes’ Principle

An immersed body experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces.

Intuition: Pressure rises with depth; the deeper bottom surface of an object feels a larger force than its top, creating a net upward force.

Representative Applications:

  • Ships & Submarines: Hull displaces water weight equal to the vessel's weight.
  • Hydrometers: Float depth directly measures a liquid's density.
  • Hot-Air Balloons: Displace heavier, cooler air to generate lift.
  • Purity Tests: Determine an object's density by measuring displaced fluid.

3. Pascal’s Law

A change of pressure applied to an incompressible fluid in a completely enclosed container is transmitted undiminished in all directions.

Intuition: Because liquids are almost incompressible, pushing at one point instantly “pushes” every other point equally.

Representative Applications:

  • Hydraulic Press & Lifts: Small input force creates a large output force.
  • Vehicle Braking Systems: Pedal force is multiplied to clamp brake pads.
  • Injection Moulding: Uniform pressure fills intricate mould cavities.
  • Heavy Machinery: Powers excavators, steering, and control actuators.

Key Distinctions Summarized

Bernoulli connects energy conservation in moving fluids.

Archimedes is about force balance due to static pressure gradient in fluids at rest.

Pascal concerns how pressure itself behaves in a confined, static fluid—and how that lets us multiply force.
